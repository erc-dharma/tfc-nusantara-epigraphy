1v3 ṅaranyan· NNakada 1986 • Understandṅaran nyan?

1v5 Er baraṅan· NNakada 1986 • Cf. Air Baraṅan in Mantyasih inscriptions. — 1v5 saṁ parānya NNakada 1986 • Read saparānya? The apparent cecak can be considered accidental. Cf. Brandes 1913: 59, № XXXVII mangilala drabya haji saparānya sadeçānya; OJODDV p. 59 saparanya.

2r2 soAṁ-soAṁ (lu)- ◇ soAṁ-soAṁ [, lu]- NNakada 1986 • The lu is still discernible, and the space before it is well preserved, without showing any trace of a punctuation sign.

Translation by Arlo Griffiths

Hail! Elapsed Śaka year 728, month of Māgha, ninth of the waxing fortnight, Hariyaṅ, Umanis, Thursday. At that time Rakai Patapān Pu Manuku demarcated the land of Muṇḍuan and of Haji Huma, given as apanage to his servant Patoran. His royal corvée will be to guard the goats … the extent of the kuramvit of the holy insignia of Patapān down to the future’s future. The name is ‘The place of guarding of the goats by the apanage holder’. And so he made a dwelling at the vindu-vindus of the land. That is the reason that the dwelling is called ‘Vala Vindu’. Thereupon he was given the grant that [the land] may not be entered by the Er Baraṅan, and by all kinds of collectors … of buying and selling. Especially those who have [a record of] good or bad deeds, may the [land] not be entered [by them]. Thus was the grant of Raka Patapān Pu Manuku to his servant Patoran, and to all who reside in Vala Vindu. If there are who disturb this grant, their reward into the future’s future is [that of] the five great sins

The minister of Patapān at that time was the Kayumvuṅan [called] Rupyan; the Mantyasih, servant of the Rakai Patapān, was the one [named] Palindua; the Partaya was the one [named] Pagəh. All of them were given gifts of 1 dhāraṇa of silver, 1 yugala of cloth per person. For the Lupaṇḍak(s) of the Er Baraṅan: 8 māṣas of gold; 1 yugala of cloth per person. For the Wahuta Pətir and the Paṇḍakyan: 1 dhāraṇa of silver, 1 yugala of cloth per person. For the 4 pituṅtuṅs of the [two villages], 4 māṣas of silver per person. The headman of Muṇḍuan [named] Bunā, the kalima [named] Sruka, the juru murəṅ [named] Klat, the … [named] Kulinā, the daṇḍa murəṅ [named] Andhani, the kaṇḍaṅan lavəs [named] Tija; the kalima of Haji Huma [named] Sruva, the juru siniṅ, parvuvus, the maṇḍər paraṅ [named] Guṇī: all of them they were given gifts in silver and cloth in accordance with their efforts. Minaṅa, the scribe of Rakai Patapān who wrote the … copper-decree, was given 8 māṣa of silver, 1 yugala of cloth.

Commentary

1v3The noun kuramwit must lay at the basis of the place name Kuramwitan attested in a few inscriptions; perhaps it is derived from an (unattested) base kamwit. Connection with keramat? There is a word kerambit meaning ‘small sickle’ in Malay.

2r1-2r3On the toponyms figuring here, see de Casparis 1950: 151–160.

2r4-2r5Note that the two kalimas bear the names Sruka and Sruva, which are evidently (derived from) the Sanskrit words for two types of ritual spoons, the sruk and the sruva. This parallelism of names suggests a strong connection of some sort between the two persons in question, despite their affiliation with two different villages.
