    Apparatus

    Textpart

    1r1// • the usual double daṇḍa with curl
    1r4sava(ḥ) [ka]hadyanan·• the reading is supported by the occurrence of the same words in Kinewu, line 5.
    1r6(husaḍar)·• maybe huvusan·, as in Telang REF?
    1r8soAra_niṁ• or read soĀra_niṁ?

    Textpart

    1v2I [bhaṭāra I vihā]ra I da(lina)n· [deya]nya mavai(ḥ) mana/ṅa\hana I pa(r)masan·• readings and restorations established by comparison with Sangsang 1r10–11 and Wukajana II 1v8
    1v3[bhaṇḍihalā](di)• this may be the earliest attestation of this word. The spelling, with bh or m, cannot be ascertained, but it seems the former predominates in the records of the Sindok period, while the latter comes into vogue later. On this basis, I restore bh
    1v3–4hīṁ-hīṁṅana (kvaiḥ)ha(nya) [paṇḍai mas·, pa](ṇḍ)ai [v](si)[,]• restored after the parallel in Sangsang 1r12
    1v9[Ikanaṁ ma](ña)mvul·, [mañavriṁ maṅapus·, maṁlākha, U]ṇḍahagi• the lacunae are filled by comparison with Sangsang 1v4
    1v11[sadūman· Umarā I saṁ makmitan· sīma, maṅkana rasa saṁ hya]ṁ Ājña haji• the lacuna is filled by comparison with Sangsang 1v6, although the word rasa saṁ hyaṁ are not found there; we need them to accommodate the visible anusvāra, and find them in Anjatan r10. The resulting number of restored akṣaras, however, is considerably higher than the number of akṣaras in the corresponding space in line 1v10, so the restoration is somewhat uncertain
    1v11–12⟦ca. 30 aks.⟧ ⟦ca. 3 akṣ.⟧• restore after Sangsang 1v6–7 masamvyavahāra hana ṅkāna maṅasəAkan· samgat· lamva pasamvaḥ I? But the correspondence of this passage between the two inscriptions does not seem to have been sufficiently precise anymore to warrant relying only on the parallel

    On the basis of significant correspondences of contents, the two Wereldmuseum plates and the one Amsterdam plate are here assumed to form parts of a single original set, despite small differences of dimensions, absence of any evident connection between the sources of acquisition, and absence of evident continuity of the text between WM-49506 (plate 2) and TM-856-2 (plate 4), which is explained here by postulating loss of a third plate. It is suspected that the three plates, together with the closely related Hujung Galuh or Sangsang plate (TM-856-1), all derive from a single trove (which would have been a kind of monastic archive), that would have become split up either before or after some or all of the plates being transported to the Netherlands and subsequently donated to two Dutch Museums.
