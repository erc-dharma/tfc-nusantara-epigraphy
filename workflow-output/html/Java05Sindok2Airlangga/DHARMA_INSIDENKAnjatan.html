Apparatus

3r1 kaR̥(ṁR̥)ṅan· • Cf. Baru (23), Gandhakuti (3r4).
3r4riṅ añjatān· • That this toponym should be read añjatān· and not ṅañjatān· is demonstrated by the reference to a rakyrān· Añjatan in the Dalinan charter (4r3).
4r5 kalpi (sa)ma marāśraya • Alternative readings, all equally possible as long as better photographs are unavailable, include kalpi(ka) mamarāśraya, kalpi(ta) mamarāśraya, kalpikā(ra) mamarāśraya or kalpikāra (sa)ma marāśraya.

As the venerable congregation (ḍaṅ saṅgha) of Amr̥tamaṅgala has exclusive authority over all their royal revenues. The arrangement shall be that they are to be divided in three.

4r3-4r11The status of the village of Añjatan ceased to be thāni bala↓16 of Girim Baṅi. Likewise the paddy-field land of the Aṅin-aṅin group, including their orchards of 1 blah: after the charter had been read aloud, the status of the land of the Aṅin-aṅin group ceased outright, in that without delay the holy cloister of Amr̥tamaṅgala would have exclusive authority over all of it, including all its valleys and hills (lbak-vukir),↓17 to begin with the various paddy fields. Now as for the course of action to be taken by the village of Añjatan, including the kalpis who take refuge,↓18 is that all of them, without exception, should keep in mind all previous arrangements, should carry out royal corvée every day on what is dilapidated of the beauty of the holy cloister of Amr̥tamaṅgala, every Manis.↓19 It extended to the course of action to be taken by the Piṅhai, the Makurug and the villagers of Girim Baṅi, including all of the neighboring villages of the village of Añjatan, and first of all the ones granted by the Great King to have the function of Girim Baṅi, including those who will be allowed to serve as Girim Baṅi in the future,↓20 for its former status (tattvabhūta) was that of a village (thāni) of the district of Girim Baṅi.↓21 Likewise the officiant of Aṅin-aṅin at this time as well as the future officiants of Aṅin-aṅin: all of them are begged to increase the perfection of the Great King’s foundation of the holy cloister of Amr̥tamaṅgala, to be based on the absence of their actions to disturb the village of Añjatan including its paddy-fields as well as the paddy-field land of the Aṅin-aṅin group, particularly its orchard of 1 blah, so that guilt of grave negligence will not befall them. Because of the fact that the substance of the holy royal charter is already irrevocable, as a confirmatory document regarding the demarcation of the holy cloister of Amr̥tamaṅgala having 1 tampah of paddy-fields as well as the paddy-field land of the Aṅin-aṅin group including its orchard of 1 blah, and particularly all of its valleys and hills. Such was the substance of the holy royal charter, a confirmatory document regarding the demarcation by the Great King of the village of Añjatan together with the paddy-fields of the Aṅin-aṅin group.

Notes

↑1. This term seems only to be found in two charters of the Airlangga period.
↑2. I adopt the interpretation of the term sukha-duḥkha itself, and of the meanings of the items in the following list, from work in progress by Timothy Lubin.
↑3. The expressions mati tibā and mati katibā both occur in various periods.
↑4. Although the spelling typically seems to be kalǝbū in this context, the required meaning is that recorded in Zoetmulder 1982: s.v. lǝbu.
↑5. The word patiga is not attested epigraphically, and not evidently suitable in this context; the reading is a bit uncertain, but I see no better option.
↑6. The term pakajaṅan is unattested but must be derived from kajaṅ.
↑7. The word pahiḍanan is of uncertain derivation and the meaning is unknown; it might be possible to read pahiḍaran, and it it conceivable to emend pahiḍaṅan.
↑8. It is extremely rare to find bantal or pabantalan quantitied by any other term than kalima. But the phrase vuṅkuḍu kasaṅa bantal in the Jeru-jeru charter of the time of Sindok (line A18) proves that ogther quantifier could occur in such contexts.
↑9. The form mañarub seems to be an irregular derivation from base tarub. It is unrecorded in Zoetmulder 1982: s.v. tarub, but found in analogous context, where application of Zoetmulder 1982: s.v. carub, añarub ‘to upset the proper order’ is unsuitable, in the Garaman charter (975 Śaka) and in three charters of early Majapahit (Balawi, Warunggahan, Tuhanyaru) and in the Pūrvādhigama. Perhaps the form has its origin in a context precisely such as the present one were several form with mañ- follow.
↑10. The form mamuṅkuḍu is not found in any other source. I presume that the common expression manūla vuṅkudu is an explanation of the former.
↑11. Or can we read baṅśi/vāṅśī and translate ‘flute’?
↑12. In the Garaman charter (3v8), we read in the parallel context mamulaṅ vlut, which Boechari 2012: page 511 translated ‘catchers of eels’. (The same expression figures as maṅulaṅ vlut in the Balawi charter, 7v2.) Boechari’s translation seems adequate, although a specific technique of cathing may have been intended. Based on the meanings indicated in Zoetmulder 1982: s.v. pulaṅ I (‘mixed with, becoming one, united in, full of, smeared with’), one might guess that the technique involved application of some kind of poisonous substance. Under pulaṅ II, the dictionary refers to Balinese pulaṅ = tiba and cites the expression apulaṅ maṅgar ‘to cast anchor’, which might suggest a rather different technique for catching eels.
↑13. Neither kapahayvan nor the form pahayu that it implies are recorded in Zoetmulder 1982. See also 4r5.
↑14. Similar passages are found in the Kusambyan charter (945 Śaka) and several other contemporary inscriptions, none of them concerned with a Buddhist institution. The term sa-mananā ‘everything that is broken’ it must be a calque on Sanskrit jīrṇa. See Zoetmulder 1982: s.v. nanā, citing Sārasamuccaya (388.3) jīrṇānanā sahananya. It is tempting to speculate that the use of the term sa-pariṣkāra, which is not found in any of the parallel passages, and which seems to fit into the sentence only with difficulty, is due to the specific Buddhist connotation of this term (Edgerton 1953: s.v. pariṣkāra). But the specifically Buddhist sense of ‘a monk’s personal utensils’ is hard to fit into the sentence.
↑15. Cf. Epigraphia Balica, “Koperplaten van de Pura Pamrajan Raja Purana te Klungkung (Bandjar Sangguhan)", 994 Śaka, 2r2–3: kunaṅ yātan hana kapaṭa i prasāstinya.
↑16. The use of the term thāni bala is otherwise found only in two inscriptions of the Singasari period (Sarvadharma and Air Asih).
↑17. This expression, found in several Kediri and Majapahit-period inscriptions, is found once in an inscription of the Sindok period (Tija & Haru-Haru, date lost but closely similar to Cunggrang of 851 Śaka): kevala buyut amabaki juga pramāṇa ri (2r4) salbak-vukirnya, savaḥnya, kəbvanya, **sapaniskāranya** kabeḥ, tka ri sukhaduḥkhanya magəm aḍmit·, tuhun tan kola(2r5)h-ulaha Ikaṁ sīma tija haru-haru denikaṁ Avaju ri manayuti, Apan huvus pulu lpas·.
↑18. The reading and word division are uncertain here. Neither the word kalpi, tentatively accepted here, nor the word kalpita from one of the alternative readings proposed in the apparatus, areattested in a any Old Javanese source, while the only word kalpika recorded in Zoetmulder 1982 means ‘ring’ and is not fitting here in that meaning. From parallel passages, we expect a term designating a kind of person. See Barsahan (r7–8) tka ri suka-duḥkhanikanaṁ rāma jātaka sama marāśraya Irikeṁ sīma I barsahan· and Sima Anglayang (4r7–4v1). We speculate that the meaning intended here is that of the specifically Buddhist term kalpikāra, namely ‘ some kind of servant of monks in a temple or monastery’ (Edgerton 1953: s.v. kalpikāra), and leave open the possibility that this very word was intended, in which case it would have to be restored by emendation. Other possibilities include reading kalpika, also a Buddhist technical term, which normally designates suitable or proper monk’s paraphernalia or suitable places for preparing monk’s food (Edgerton 1953: s.v. kalpika, kalpikaśālā), but could conceivably have designated in this context categories of people suitable for monks’ frequentation.  Least plausible among the alternative seems to be the reading kalpita, which exists in Sanskrit but never in any meaning that appears suitable in the present context.
↑19. The indications pratidina ‘every day’ and aṅkǝn manis ‘every Manis’ seem contradictory, but presumably one is to read them together to get the meaning ‘every Manis day (of the 5-day week)’.
↑20. Similar expressions are found with some frequency in the Airlangga corpus. See e.g. Kusambyan (c26–28) saṅ anāgata vineḥ madaṇḍər· ṅuniveḥ Ikanaṁ vineḥ ma(k)urug·.
↑21. The term tattvabhūta was so far attested epigraphically only in an inscription of late Majapahit, the Waringin Pitu charter (7v2), in a similar type of sentence: maṅkana pva tatvabhūta saṁ hyaṁ dharmma riṁ rājasakusumapura.
