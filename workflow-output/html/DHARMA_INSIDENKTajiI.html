    Commentary

    The text contains two dates: the standard date of issue preceded by svasti śakavarṣātīta (convertible to 8 April 901), and the following day preceded by kahlamanya on 6v6 (convertible to 9 April 901). This indicates that the issuing of the charter and the accompanying ceremonies were treated as historical events and were recorded retrospectively.

    On the ceremonies involving the sima stones, see Titi Surti Nastiti (2018): 189.

    1r2. The toponym Taji is located in the vatek Demung, which otherwise appears only in the Wulakan inscription (according to ROEJ). Unfortunately, the provenance of Wulakan is not clear either, and the use of the toponym Taji there is not enlightening as to its location.

    3r7. Toponym buravan, cf. vuravan of the Airlangga inscriptions.

    7r7. The royal sanctuary parhyaṅan haji iṅ Raja is mentioned in the R̥ṣiśāsana (ed. Schoettel).

    OJO preamble: “Eveneens teekende hij [i.e. Brandes] aan, dat de tweede plaat waarschijnlijk no. 3 zou zijn, en de derde no. 6 (“mogelijk is 6 na haḍangan het cijfer van de plaat”), terwijl reeds bekend was, dat de hierbij aansluitende vierde plaat het cijfer 7 droeg.”

    Four plates from an original total of seven. The extant plates are numbered 1, 3, 6, and 7. Plates no. 1, 3, and 6 are each inscribed on one face only, which we designate recto. Plate no. 7 is inscribed on both sides.

    This inscription bears certain similarities with the recently discovered Plered plate, which is the fourth in a set. These include resemblances of hand, the similarity of plate dimensions, and the use of plate numbering, which is unusual in the Balitung corpus. There are, however, too many differences to be able to confidently assert that the Plered plate might be the fourth plate in the Taji set: different sizes of the akṣaras, textual discontinuity between the end of Taji plate 3 and Plered, and differences of titulature. We cautiously propose that Taji and Plered may represent two different texts produced by the same scribe or in the same workshop.

    The “,” punctuation sign takes the shape of a small raised circle, sometimes unclosed at the bottom. This feature makes it difficult to distinguish between the punctuation and the numeral 0. We have made the editorial assumption that numerals are not followed by punctuation, hence we read 0 instead of punctuation in ambiguous cases. For example, we read ron 10 instead of ron 1, in 6v3.

    The syllable ru and the numeral 4 are not clearly distinguished in this scribal hand. We read either ru or 4 according to the demands of sense and context.
